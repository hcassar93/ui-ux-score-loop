# UI/UX Principle Notes

Compact scoring guidance distilled from the user's UI/UX playbook. Use this file when scoring views and choosing improvements. Do not quote the book; apply these ideas to the user's product context.

## Visual Hierarchy

Visual hierarchy is the ordering of attention. A strong view makes the most important information and action obvious first, then lets supporting details recede. Score highly when size, position, color, contrast, weight, and spacing all guide the user toward the next meaningful action without shouting every element at once.

Watch for misplaced emphasis. Large images, bold labels, bright secondary buttons, oversized metadata, or equally styled actions can make the wrong thing feel important. Before improving a view, rank what the user needs most, then make that information or action visually dominant while reducing the pull of low-value details.

## Proximity

Proximity tells users what belongs together. Related labels, fields, helper text, actions, and content should sit close enough to be read as one unit, while unrelated groups need enough separation to feel distinct. Score highly when the grouping is understandable without explanation.

Poor proximity increases hesitation because the user has to infer relationships. In forms, labels should clearly belong to their fields; in dashboards, controls should sit near the data they affect; in cards, metadata should attach to the right object. Use spacing to reduce cognitive load, not just to decorate.

## Clarity

Clarity is the user's ability to understand what a screen is, what each element means, and what to do next. A clear UI uses readable text, obvious navigation, familiar labels, and enough context to prevent confusion. Score highly when the user can proceed without guessing.

Clarity is not the same as minimalism. Oversimplified screens can remove needed context, while overloaded screens bury the task in options. Penalize misleading instructions, ambiguous copy, unexplained icons, raw errors, hidden next steps, and designs where the agent understands the interface only because it can infer intent.

## Alignment

Alignment creates order. Text, controls, cards, images, and data should follow consistent axes so the page feels intentional and easy to scan. Left alignment is usually best for reading, right alignment helps compare numbers, and center alignment should be reserved for short titles or focused moments.

Score down when elements drift, when similar groups use different alignment rules, or when centered text creates awkward reading paths. Grids, auto-layout, repeated column rules, and consistent alignment patterns make a UI feel calmer and more professional.

## Contrast

Contrast separates things so users can read, compare, and act. It can come from color, size, weight, borders, outlines, shadows, or background treatment. Score highly when important text is legible, primary actions stand out, interactive states are visible, and accessibility is preserved.

Contrast should solve a purpose, not just add drama. Low contrast harms readability, but excessive or inconsistent contrast can create false hierarchy. For text over images or dynamic backgrounds, use overlays, blur, tint, or framing so content remains readable across real content variations.

## Simplicity

Simplicity means keeping the interface focused on what the user needs to accomplish now. A simple view removes irrelevant details, redundant controls, noisy imagery, and competing information so the task feels approachable. Score highly when the user can understand the screen quickly and act without sorting through clutter.

Do not confuse simplicity with stripping away useful context. Keep what supports confidence, decision-making, and recovery. In improvement loops, remove or demote low-value details before adding new UI, and make sure every visible element earns its place in the flow.

## Whitespace

Whitespace is the empty space that gives information room to breathe. It improves readability, focus, grouping, and perceived polish. Score highly when sections feel spacious enough to scan, related elements are grouped consistently, and the layout does not feel cramped or visually noisy.

Use whitespace systematically. A spacing scale such as multiples of 4 or 8 helps the design feel intentional and makes related/unrelated distances predictable. Random spacing values, overly tight groups, and inconsistent padding make the interface feel unstable even when the content is correct.

## Layout

Layout is the structure that joins usability and visual presentation. A strong layout reflects what matters to the user, places information in the order a human needs it, and makes the intended path easy to follow. Score highly when the arrangement supports the job to be done, not merely a tidy composition.

Assess whether the order, grouping, and format fit the task. Product pages may need images before details, comparison choices may work better as cards than lists, and forms can become more intuitive when their structure mirrors the final output. Creative layouts are useful only when they make information easier to understand or act on.

## Balance And Harmony

Balance and harmony describe how the whole screen feels when all elements work together. A balanced UI gives every element appropriate prominence without letting one image, button, color, or panel overpower the rest. Score highly when the design feels coherent, calm, and easy to understand as a whole.

Look at proportion, scale, color distribution, spacing, typography, and element relationships together. A screen can have good hierarchy but still feel off if one visual device dominates or if the palette is monotonous. Improve by tuning relative emphasis, not by adding decoration.

## Consistency

Consistency lets users learn once and apply that knowledge everywhere. Buttons, inputs, cards, icons, states, spacing, typography, and interaction patterns should behave and look predictably across screens and repeated items. Score highly when similar things look similar and different things only differ for a clear reason.

Consistency does not mean sameness. Product images, content, and states can vary, but the system around them should remain stable. Watch for mixed icon styles, inconsistent corner radii, uneven card heights, changing button colors, and repeated components that imply different meanings accidentally.

## Visual Cues

Visual cues help users understand faster than text alone. Icons, images, avatars, logos, initials, color markers, progress indicators, and state treatments can make a view more memorable and easier to parse. Score highly when cues clarify identity, status, direction, grouping, or affordance.

Cues must be meaningful and consistent. Decorative icons that add no comprehension create noise, while mismatched cue styles weaken trust. Prefer cues that reduce reading, reveal state, show progress, or help users recognize people, places, actions, and next steps at a glance.

## Depth And Texture

Depth and texture create layers, affordance, and focus. Shadows, outlines, background shifts, elevation, blur, and subtle material effects can make interactive or floating elements easier to understand. Score highly when depth clarifies what is above, clickable, active, or separated from the page.

Use depth with restraint and a system. Soft, background-aware shadows usually feel more polished than harsh generic shadows, and modals/dropdowns need more elevation than ordinary cards. Trend effects such as glassmorphism are useful only when they preserve readability, accessibility, and brand fit.

## Color Theory

Color sets mood, communicates brand, and guides interaction. Score highly when primary color is used sparingly for important actions or links, neutral backgrounds let content breathe, status colors follow conventions, and light/dark modes both remain legible and coherent.

Too much color weakens meaning. If everything uses the brand color, nothing feels primary. Do not rely on color alone for errors, success, warnings, selected states, or required actions; pair color with text, icons, shape, or position so the UI remains understandable across vision differences and lighting conditions.

## Typography

Typography carries both meaning and tone. A strong typographic system uses readable fonts, limited font families, clear heading/body relationships, comfortable line length, adequate line height, and suitable weights. Score highly when users can scan, read, and distinguish hierarchy without strain.

Avoid typography that looks stylish at the expense of comprehension. Do not use too many fonts, extra-light body text, tiny sizes, pure black/white text everywhere, low contrast, centered long paragraphs, or oversized line lengths. Use headings, subheadings, paragraph breaks, color, size, and weight to make information digestible.

## Interaction Cost

Interaction cost is the mental, physical, and time effort required to complete the goal. Score highly when the user can recognize options, make decisions, act with few steps, avoid repetition, recover from mistakes, and understand progress without waiting in uncertainty.

Reduce cost by keeping related actions close, making targets easy to hit, minimizing choices, chunking information, removing distractions, preferring recognition over recall, streamlining steps, and exposing important options instead of hiding them in menus. Count waiting, reading, scrolling, typing, remembering, searching, and error recovery as real cost.
